Mixed Reality Assisted Design

Design Goal

Using mental imagery, designers often synthesize design ideas in their minds before translating them to a physical or a digital medium. Although it is not an actual image, it is a representation of physical reality in the working memory. Mental synthesis of a design idea is achieved by complex manipulations of mental imagery. As the complexity of the design increases, the number and nature operations required on mental imagery also increases. As a result, the mental effort used in the working memory (cognitive load) increases. Some say that externalizing this process using design tools such as paper or computer aided design (CAD) software can reduce the cognitive load. However, recent studies prove that such two dimensional interfaces are not compatible with mental imagery operations; and therefore, they not very effective in reducing cognitive load.

With the advent of augmented reality (AR), we have finally a tool that could truly enable designers to work in three dimensions. Industry experts predict that almost all of future modeling and design will be done in immersive three dimensional environment. Therefore, the effectiveness of AR enabled design interface is of great practical importance to the design industry. The main goal of this project is to develop an AR enabled design interface; and use it to study its effectiveness as a design tool.

The road to design

Research and Analysis

The conceptual framework used to test the effectiveness of an AR enabled design interface is the Finke’s creative invention task. In this task, a participant is asked to construct a new object/design from a subset of primitives shown in the figure below.

A participant has to combine a given subset of primitives, to design a novel shape without changing the basic shape of the primitive. By doing so, this task tests the participants' ability to create, inspect, and restructure mental imagery by externalizing it into an AR interface. For example, see how three basic shapes are used to design an umbrella object/design.

Target users of this interface are designers. For the purpose of understanding their needs, the data elicitation activity was broadly divided into two phases. In the first phase, a literature review was undertaken to understand a designer’s work domain and issues. In the second phase, designers were interviewed to collect work activity data.

Contextual analysis was done by building a work activity affinity diagram (WAAD). The chief objective of contextual analysis was to identify common work patterns and thereby extract of functional requirements. For constructing a WAAD, first, work activity notes were synthesized from the raw work activity data. Then, the work activity notes were grouped into preliminary sections based on their content. Related sections were then joined to form clusters. Finally, clusters were further refined and labelled. Requirements

The following are the functional requirements of the interface that was extracted after contextual analysis:

Users shall be able to grab the primitives using hand gestures.

Users shall be able to effectively use gaze, hand gestures, and voice commands to manipulate the primitives.

Users shall be able to effectively change location, orientation, and the scale of the primitives.

Users shall be able to learn and use the application with minimal training.

Users shall be able to manipulate the primitives without having to use a visual menu or commands.

Users shall be able to undo any previous action.

Users shall be able to complete Finke’s creative invention task under two minutes.

Design Informing Models

A physical model was constructed to give a physical setting to the real and the virtual content. It also shows the physical dimensions of the work environment and interaction space. A storyboard was also created to document the designer’s mental model. This storyboard documents the designer’s vision of the system and gives an overview of what it does.

Design Production

Pilot Test

Rapid iterative testing and evaluation (RITE) technique was used to evaluate the design. This technique could be classified as a quasi-empirical evaluation technique, as it collects qualitative data and gives a researcher the freedom and flexibility in terms of evaluation goals and approaches. Six graduate students were recruited as participants based on their design experience. Qualitative data were collected using think aloud protocol. After every experimental session, a severity was assigned to problems/issues that were identified. Based on the severity, high priority issues were fixed before the next session. This process was iterated for all of the six participants.

Lessons Learned

The optimal placement distance of volumetric content is between 1 and 1.5 meters. However, most of the academic literature recommends two meters. Often, users find the placement of content at two meters quite frustrating, and have to move the content closer to them.

The interface needs to provide cues that compensate for haptic feedback. That is, provide visual and auditory cues that are synchronized with user’s gestural and voice input. The more it looks like they are interacting with the real content, the better.

The interface has to support the limited capacity of working memory. That is, limit the number of virtual content or primitives, so that the users do not have difficulty mapping and spatially localizing the virtual content.

Gestures provided out of the box from MS HoloLens require practice; and, therefore, slows down the interaction speed and increases the learning curve. For example, the tap and hold gesture of the MS HoloLens takes practice and it is not as similar as the universal grabbing gesture.

Voice commands kept the interface simple and effective. Additionally, voice commands reduces the need for fatiguing arm motions. It can also theoretically reduce cognitive load, but this needs to be verified using empirical data. Also, it provides a method of interacting with the volumetric content without using on-screen visual controls.

Rotation of the volumetric content in all three directions, at the same time, requires a complex series of fatiguing gestures. It is best to constraint rotation of objects to a single axis, as it allows the user to control the degree of rotation in a much more precise fashion.

The ability to reset the interface/application was appreciated. This made the participant feel that they were in complete control, and could restore a previous state if needed, and undo any changes at will.
